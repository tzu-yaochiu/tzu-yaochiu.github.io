My name is Tzu-Yao Chiu. I'm a 4th year PhD student at The Ohio State University working with [Dr. Julie D. Golomb](https://psychology.osu.edu/people/golomb.9). My research focuses on eye movements, more specifically the cognitive and neural mechanisms supporting our real-life perceptual experience. My work combines behavioral psychophysics, electroencephalogram (EEG), functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), and cognitive modeling. Feel free to reach out to me if you have any questions! 

### A bit more about me
My research journey began when I was an undergrad at National Chengchi University, Taiwan, where I did an independent research project in the Eye Movements and Reading Lab with [Dr. Jie-Li Tsai](https://emrlab.nccu.edu.tw/en/). This was where I learned a great deal about eye movements (like the fact that we make them so often), and gradually fell in love with working with the eye-tracker. I then moved across the globe to pursue a MS degree at University of Southampton, UK, during which I worked with my great advisor [Dr. Denis Drieghe](https://www.southampton.ac.uk/people/5x6gmg/professor-denis-drieghe). We did a fun and exciting project on the role of visual crowding in eye movements during reading, where we examined the influences of text spacing on many aspects of people's eye movements (e.g., fixation time, fixation location). During this time, I also worked with [Dr. Erich Graf](https://www.southampton.ac.uk/people/5wzxpy/doctor-erich-graf) on ensemble coding and integration. By the end of my degree, I realized that I was actually quite fond my basic visision research (as opposed to psycholinguistics). 

For my grad school, I moved (again) to The Ohio State University, US, to work in the Vision and Cognitive Neuroscience Lab with [Dr. Julie D. Golomb](https://psychology.osu.edu/people/golomb.9), and I've been here for four years now. My current research tackles the fundamental question: How do we maintain stable perceptual experience across eye movements? I tackle this question from a variety of angles: (1) how do eye movements influence the deployment of attention, and what are the factors modulating these effects; (2) how do we measure implicit cognitive/neural processes around the time of eye movements, and what do they tell us about mechanisms for stable perception; (3) what are the stability mechanisms supporting perception in naturalistic environments. I've also learned to combine new approaches, including electroencephalogram (EEG), functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), and cognitive modeling in my research. If you're interested in any of these studies, you can find bits of them in the publication page.
